Ah windy city, the crown jewel of midwestern festivities. I come in the bewildering month of May, the typical is-it-summer-yet-type identity crisis lurking around the corner, misty mornings, a glimpse of sun over Millenium Park, already buzzing with picnic enthusiasts, admirers of steel structures, young couples doing that one trick shot of pintching the bean in between their fingers, *fruteros* selling sliced up mangos and watermelons (Tajín optional), and the illusion of a much warmer weather. Pritzker Pavilion still fenced up, its dendritic branches visible, silently gathering intelligence against the backdrop of the eastside skyline, which is comprised primarily of - you guessed it - insurance giants, the likes of Blue Cross Blue Shield, Prudential and Aon, a reserved affinity for corporate America running deep here in the heartland, the sincerity of which is said to have paralyzed more than a dozen east coast leftists.

I hopped on the Metra, or was it the CTA? What's the difference now, but a rumbling reminder of another has-been industry squandered by the relentless rise of the CME and commodity trading.  Double deckered, aisle open to the above, tinted glasses letting in splashes of the afternoon sun in what has quietly become a shower now? Uh-oh. Conductor coming through, dressed exactly as one would envision in period-accurate pieces, which is to say congruent to that of LIRR or amtrak, tickets examined, scanned, punched, and pleasantries exchanged. The train car seated no more than 6 passengers, myself included, South Side residents who seemed to have been recently reunited by a downtown brunch encounter, sterotypical ChicagoU students, scheming away in sterotypical ChicagoU language, you know, getting too comfortable with the word 'orthogonal' and stuff. I watched them get off at Hyde Park, disappearing soon into the distant green.

Griffin Museum of Science and Industry. If it isn't Mr. Citadel coming to rescue the neighborhood sweetheart. A wonderful concoction of environmental activism, marginally informational replicas of mining facilities, Buster Keaton playing in a make-belief movie theater of Main St. USA of yesteryear, a Burlington Zephyr converted to an infodesk. I found myself onboard a captured German U-505 submarine, confined in a grayed out basement just steps off of the SpiderVerse exhibition, having sunk 8 ships of the Allied Forces over 12 patrols across the Atlantic, almost scuttled at the command of *Oblt.z.S.* Harald Lange, bids farewells to the familiar shores of Western Africa, and finds herself in the navy base of Bermuda, before permanently resting here in illinois, not without the $250,000 raised by local residents for her transport.
