Conveniently located in the culinary vacuum that is Midtown South (unless your go-to diet is a combination of corporate slop and dry-aged steaks, in which case, kudos to your digestive system), Pizzarte has effectively communicated its ambition for mediocrity through its abomination of a name. But I've seen worse. "The Italian Restaurant" is a fierce competitor on the UWS. Who are the customers of this hole-in-the-wall, semi-basement establishment that could easily have been converted to a luxury studio apartment? I'd imagine stranded tourists displaced from their natural habitat of the 2-mile radius around Times Square and Penn Station, patrons of Carnegie Hall catching a post-concert bite (those who can't afford a regular visit to the Russian Tea Room, anyway), and me — for whatever inexplicable reason, this marks my third annual visit, out of my own volition.

I must confess I am no connoisseur of pizzalogy. I'd be able to tell a New York slice from a Neapolitan pie (which happens to be the specialty here), sure, but do I have a Portnoyian scale ranking these Italian flatbreads by crisp, flop, dough texture, or cheese quality? No sir. A pizza is a pizza — one of those archetypes of food so thoroughly perfected through generational effort that even a crass industrial rendition on the other side of the planet (looking at you, Pizza Hut China) is good. No notes. So naturally, in a city where quality two-dollar slices are readily available up and down the block, you expect a pretty damn good pizza paying thirty-five dollars for a personal pie. And yes, I am sure the parmigiano reggiano is freshly produced by in-house cattle, the pizza oven built brick by brick from the ruins of the Colosseum, the tomato sauce so freshly puréed it practically glows with the Sicilian sun. Still.

We've consistently ordered two pies, a white and a red. The nights have consistently been punctuated by the Italian that the owners — they have to be — sporadically produce over generous pours of wine from the old country. A passion project for sure, the kind Bourdain famously disdained while the rest of us helplessly romanticized about. I've watched the older gentleman enter on multiple occasions, beaming with Mafia boss energy — or that of a hedge fund operator, the two asymptotically converging — greet the bartender, small-talk with guests, settle into his designated spot, greet the bartender again, then gradually shed his American pleasantries as the night descends, until completely consumed by the Italian inside. One can relate to such sentiments a few times throughout the years. Maybe that's why I keep coming back.
